Introduction and Rotation Overview

Welcome to your neuroanesthesiology rotation

Welcome

Welcome to your neuroanesthesiology rotation! This is your time to grow and take ownership of your learning. We can't do it for you, but we're here to support, guide, and cheer you on. We're glad to be part of your journey.

Rotation Structure

The neuroanesthesiology rotation consists of four weeks dedicated to the perioperative care of neurosurgical patients. You will:

  • Participate in 3-4 neurosurgical cases per week
  • Attend weekly neurosurgical conference
  • Complete one academic project related to neuroanesthesia
  • Maintain a case log with reflections
  • Receive mid-rotation and end-rotation feedback

Review your assigned cases 24 hours in advance and prepare focused prereading for each procedure.

Fundamentals of Neurophysiology

CBF regulation and anesthetic effects on the brain

Cerebral Blood Flow (CBF) Regulation

CBF is regulated by:

P

Pressure Autoregulation

Maintains constant CBF between MAP 50-150 mmHg

C

Chemical Regulation

PaCO₂ (strongest factor), PaO₂, pH

M

Metabolic Coupling

CBF increases with cerebral metabolic rate (CMRO₂)

Anesthetic Effects on Brain

Agent CBF CMRO₂ ICP Autoregulation
Propofol ↓↓ ↓↓↓ ↓↓ Preserved
Volatile Agents ↑↑ ↓↓ ↑ Impaired
Ketamine ↑↑ ↑ ↑ Variable
Dexmedetomidine ↓ ↓ ↓ Preserved
Opioids nc ↓ nc Preserved

Mnemonic

"VAPID"

  • Volatile agents: ↑CBF, ↓CMRO₂
  • Anesthetics (IV: propofol, thiopental): ↓CBF, ↓CMRO₂, ↓ICP
  • Protection: Burst suppression on EEG (barbs, propofol)
  • Increased ICP: Ketamine caution
  • Depress neuromonitoring: Volatile agents

Brain Protection Strategies

  • Maintaining adequate cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP)
  • Avoiding hyperthermia
  • Glucose control (avoid both hyper- and hypoglycemia)
  • Reducing metabolic demand (mild hypothermia, anesthetic depth)
  • Optimizing oxygenation and managing seizure risk

Neuromonitoring Principles

Common modalities and anesthetic considerations

SSEPs (Somatosensory Evoked Potentials)

Pathway: Peripheral nerve → dorsal column → thalamus → sensory cortex

Moderate anesthetic sensitivity Spine & vascular cases

MEPs (Motor Evoked Potentials)

Pathway: Motor cortex → corticospinal tract → alpha motor neurons → muscle

High anesthetic sensitivity Avoid NMB

EMG (Electromyography)

Free-running and triggered types for nerve root monitoring

Affected by NMB Spine surgery

EEG / ECoG / MER

EEG: Depth of anesthesia, seizure detection. ECoG: Direct cortical activity. MER: Single neuron activity for DBS.

MER: Extremely high sensitivity

Key Point

TIVA Advantages: Propofol-based TIVA provides stable neuromonitoring conditions. Generally avoid neuromuscular blockade during MEP and EMG monitoring.

Resident Action

Before each case, confirm with the neuromonitoring team which modalities will be used and adjust your anesthetic plan accordingly.

5

Asleep Craniotomy

Tumor resection, vascular malformations, abscess drainage

Preoperative Assessment

Neurological Evaluation

  • • Review clinic notes for deficits
  • • Document baseline function
  • • Assess seizure history

Laboratory Testing

  • • CBC, BMP, coags
  • • Type and screen
  • • Anticonvulsant levels

Special Considerations

  • • Glucose if diabetic/steroids
  • • Target 70-180 mg/dL
  • • Cardiopulmonary reserve

Anesthetic Technique

Induction & Airway

  • • Two large-bore IVs (≥18G)
  • • Consider arterial line before induction
  • • Video laryngoscopy for high ICP risk
  • • Secure ETT with tape (avoid ties obstructing venous drainage)

TIVA Maintenance (preferred for neuromonitoring)

Propofol

75-150 mcg/kg/min

Remifentanil

0.05-0.2 mcg/kg/min

Dexmedetomidine

0.3-0.7 mcg/kg/hr

Resident Tip

Anticipate hypertensive response with Mayfield pin placement. Have remifentanil bolus (0.5-1 mcg/kg) drawn up and ready.

Intraoperative Management

ICP Management

  • • Normocapnia (PaCO₂ 35-40 mmHg)
  • • Gentle hyperventilation only for acute crisis
  • • Mannitol 0.25-1 g/kg if requested
  • • Adequate depth before dural opening

Hemodynamic Goals

  • • SBP 100-140 mmHg
  • • Use phenylephrine or norepinephrine
  • • Ensure adequate venous drainage
  • • Avoid extreme neck rotation

Key Learning Points

  1. 1. ICP management combines positioning, ventilation control, and osmotic therapy
  2. 2. Hemodynamic control must balance perfusion against bleeding risk
  3. 3. Smooth emergence prevents dangerous ICP spikes
  4. 4. Early recognition of postoperative complications is critical

Awake Craniotomy

Mapping eloquent cortex during tumor resection or epilepsy surgery

Sedated-Awake-Sedated (SAS) Technique

1

Initial Sedation

Propofol + remifentanil for positioning, head fixation, craniotomy

2

Awake Phase

Minimal/no sedation during cortical mapping

3

Re-Sedation

Resume lighter sedation for closure

Intraoperative Complications

Seizures

First-line: Cold saline irrigation by surgeon. Second-line: Small propofol bolus (10-30 mg). Reserve benzodiazepines for refractory seizures.

Agitation/Anxiety

Verbal reassurance first. Small dexmedetomidine doses (0.1-0.2 mcg/kg) if needed. Consider termination if unmanageable.

Resident Tip

During the awake phase, maintain a calm demeanor and speak clearly. Your composure directly impacts patient cooperation.

Transsphenoidal Pituitary Surgery

Endoscopic approach through the sphenoid sinus

Endocrine Evaluation

Acromegaly

Difficult airway due to macroglossia and mandibular hypertrophy. Consider awake fiberoptic.

Cushing's Disease

Hypertension, hyperglycemia, difficult IV access.

Hypopituitarism

Need for stress-dose steroids (hydrocortisone 100 mg IV).

Diabetes Insipidus

Baseline fluid/electrolyte status. Monitor for postop DI.

Critical Warning

NO bag-mask ventilation post-extubation — risk of pneumocephalus due to base of skull surgical site.

Postoperative Endocrine Complications

Condition Timing Treatment
Diabetes Insipidus 12-24 hours postop DDAVP, fluid replacement
SIADH 4-7 days postop Fluid restriction

LITT (Laser Interstitial Thermal Therapy)

MRI-guided thermal ablation using NeuroBlate system

Unique Challenges

OR ↔ MRI Transport

MRI Safety

Prolonged Duration

The "Seaweed Bundle" Setup

  • • Six microbore tubings in series
  • • Total tubing volume ~10.2 mL
  • • 4-minute delay for medication changes
  • • TIVA carrier at 150 mL/hr via pump

Resident Tip

Tape microbore extensions securely to the floor or cover with blankets. Label them with red tags for visibility and to avoid trip hazards.

Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)

Electrode placement for movement disorders

Parkinson's Disease Essential Tremor Dystonia OCD (emerging)

MAO-I Interactions

Screen for MAO-I medications — may cause hypertensive crisis with vasopressors. Discuss management with your attending.

Stage 1: Electrode Placement

MAC Technique (Preferred)

  • • Midazolam 1-2 mg for headframe only
  • • Propofol 25-50 mcg/kg/min
  • • Dexmedetomidine 0.2-0.7 mcg/kg/hr
  • • SBP <130 mmHg
  • • Pause sedation during macrostimulation

GA Technique (Alternative)

  • • Video laryngoscopy if headframe in place
  • • Light TIVA for MER quality
  • • Propofol 50-100 mcg/kg/min
  • • Remifentanil 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min
  • • Avoid volatile agents

Key Learning Points

  1. 1. Medication Safety: Understand MAO-I interactions
  2. 2. Airway Management: Master headframe intubation techniques
  3. 3. Neuromonitoring: Optimize conditions for MER and macrostimulation
  4. 4. Blood Pressure: Maintain SBP <130 mmHg

Responsive Neurostimulation (RNS)

Closed-loop system for refractory focal epilepsy

RNS continuously monitors brain electrical activity and delivers stimulation when seizure patterns are detected. Primary indication: Medically refractory focal epilepsy with 1-2 well-localized foci not amenable to resection.

Seizure Precautions

Document detailed seizure history. Have propofol 10-30 mg and lorazepam 1-2 mg readily available. Highest seizure risk in first 24-48 hours post-implantation.

11

VNS Implantation

Vagal nerve stimulator for refractory epilepsy

Bradycardia Risk

Left vagus nerve placement minimizes cardiac effects but bradycardia can occur during testing. Have atropine 0.5 mg or glycopyrrolate 0.2 mg drawn up and ready.

Key Considerations

  • Secure ETT on the right side (away from surgical field)
  • TIVA preferred to minimize seizure risk
  • Monitor for paratracheal hematoma postoperatively
  • Assess vocal cord function (hoarseness indicates damage)

IEA Recording & Cortical Mapping

Interictal epileptiform activities and ECoG

Anesthetic Agent Selection

Agent Effect on IEAs Recommendation
Propofol Variable effect Stop/reduce 15-20 min before recording
Dexmedetomidine Minimal at low doses 0.3-0.5 mcg/kg/hr acceptable
Sevoflurane Nonspecific spike activity Keep <0.5 MAC if used
Remifentanil Usually acceptable 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min

Resident Tip

Inform the neurophysiologist about dosing changes at least 15 minutes before recording to account for context-sensitive half-time effects on EEG.

Complex Spine Surgery

Multilevel fusion, scoliosis correction, tumor resection

Team Huddle Topics

Blood Loss Planning

  • • Cell saver for EBL >1000 mL
  • • TXA: 10 mg/kg bolus → 1 mg/kg/hr
  • • Type/screen vs. crossmatch
  • • POC testing (TEG)

Hemodynamic Goals

  • • MAP ≥80-85 mmHg for cord manipulation
  • • Permissive hypotension if needed
  • • Phenylephrine or norepinephrine
  • • Target Hgb 8-9 g/dL

Signal Loss Protocol

  1. 1. Notify attending and surgical team immediately
  2. 2. Increase MAP to ≥85-90 mmHg
  3. 3. Check hemoglobin, oxygenation, electrolytes via ABG
  4. 4. Verify normothermia
  5. 5. Consider reducing anesthesia depth
  6. 6. Rule out technical issues with neuromonitoring team

Resident Tip

Set a timer to check the face hourly during prone cases. Document these checks in your anesthesia record.

Mnemonics and drug tables

Brain Protection

"HEADS"

  • Hyperventilation (judicious use only)
  • Euvolemia (or slight hyperosmolarity)
  • Anesthetic management (reduce CMRO₂)
  • Drugs (mannitol, hypertonic saline)
  • Suppression (EEG burst suppression)

Signs of VAE

"MACH"

  • Mill-wheel murmur
  • Air aspiration from central line
  • Cardiovascular collapse
  • Hypoxia
